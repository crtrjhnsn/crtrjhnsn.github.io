One framing correction baked into this brief

Stryker is a medical-device company, not a pharma company — and that changes almost every part of the sales story.

Not "trials"

Clearance, not clinical trials

Most products clear via the FDA 510(k) or PMA pathways, not pharma-style Phase 1–3 trials. "In development" usually means a limited market release, not a randomized endpoint readout.

Mechanical, not biologic

Mechanism is physics, not pharmacology

An implant's "mechanism of action" is materials, geometry, and fixation — not a biological pathway. Where biology matters (osseointegration, thrombus capture) the glossary says so explicitly.

Bundled, not billed

The device is rarely billed to insurance

Hospitals and ASCs are paid a bundled rate for the procedure; the implant and the robot come out of that bundle. There is no separate insurance code for "robot." This reframes the entire payer conversation — see Payer & coverage.

The portfolio

Two segments, one operating-efficiency thesis

Stryker reorganized into two reportable segments and dropped "Spine" from the Orthopaedics name in its 2024 reporting. Roughly 59% of revenue sits in MedSurg & Neurotechnology, ~41% in Orthopaedics. About 74% of sales are in the United States.

MedSurg & Neurotechnology

The capital-equipment and disposables engine. Five businesses, all oriented toward OR throughput, visualization, and provider workflow.

  • Instruments — surgical power tools, navigation, safety tech (e.g., System 9, Neptune waste management).
  • Endoscopy — cameras, video, endoscopic & communications systems (1788 platform).
  • Medical — ProCuity beds, stretchers, LIFEPAK defibrillators, emergency care.
  • Vascular — renamed from Neurovascular; now includes Inari's peripheral VTE portfolio.
  • Neuro Cranial — neurovascular coils/stents, CMF, interventional spine.

Orthopaedics

The implant franchise, increasingly sold as a system anchored by the Mako robot. This is the razor-and-blade story.

  • Knees — Triathlon family + Mako Total & Partial Knee. New Triathlon Gold for metal sensitivity.
  • Hips — Mako Total Hip, now with first-to-market robotic revision capability.
  • Trauma & Extremities — nailing systems, plating, CMF, foot & ankle, upper-extremity.
  • Other Orthopaedics — enabling technologies, Mako capital, Spinal Implants (US business divested 2025).
Why it mattersMako isn't just a product — it's the pull-through mechanism. A placed robot favors Stryker implants for years and locks in service revenue. The capital sale is the door; the disposables and implants are the annuity.

Adoption

Uptake by provider — and what each one actually cares about

Penetration is real but uneven: at year-end 2024, an estimated ~45% of global knee implant procedures and ~20% of hip procedures involving Stryker were done with Mako. The sales points differ sharply by who's buying.

Early / heavy adopters

High-volume academic & ortho-specialty hospitals

Already on Mako; the conversation is about new applications (spine, shoulder, revision) and throughput. They care about: case volume per robot, OR time, surgeon recruitment, and being seen as the regional center of excellence.

Fastest-growing

Ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs)

As primary joints migrate outpatient, ASCs want predictable, fast, low-complication cases. They care about: footprint/cost of the robot, turnover time, implant cost per case, and same-day discharge rates.

Slowest / most price-sensitive

Community hospitals & manual surgeons

May not justify a $0.5M–$1M+ robot on volume alone — the target for the lower-friction Mako RPS. They care about: capital justification, learning curve, and whether the robot pays for itself given no separate reimbursement.

The sales points doctors actually raise

What surgeons push on

  • Precision & reproducibility — CT-based plan + haptics for consistent component placement.
  • Learning curve & workflow — how many cases to proficiency; does it slow me down at first?
  • Outcomes evidence — do the data show fewer revisions, better alignment, faster recovery?
  • Ergonomics — less physical strain over a long OR day.

What administrators push on

  • Capital ROI — no incremental reimbursement, so the robot must pay via volume, length-of-stay, and complication reduction.
  • Pull-through economics — implant + service annuity over the robot's life.
  • Site-of-service fit — does it work in our ASC build-out?
  • Bundled-payment risk — does it help us hit TEAM targets and PROM benchmarks?
Accuracy flag — do not overstate

The clinical literature on robotic arthroplasty shows strong radiographic/alignment precision and short-term recovery signals, but long-term, high-grade evidence that Mako reduces revision rates versus manual is still maturing. Do not claim a hard revision-rate reduction in the field without the specific citation. Sell precision and workflow, not unproven survivorship.

Market access

Insurance coverage & the hurdles before a case gets paid

This is the most misunderstood part of the device story, so it gets the most space. The implant and the robot are almost never billed separately to a payer — they're absorbed into the facility's bundled payment for the procedure.

How payment actually flows

  • Inpatient Medicare → paid by MS-DRG (e.g., DRG 469/470 for major joint replacement). The implant cost comes out of that fixed payment.
  • Outpatient/ASC → paid via APC/facility rate under HCPCS C-codes.
  • There is no separate CPT or add-on code for robotic assistance or navigation. The robot generates no incremental revenue per case — its value is operational.

The coverage hurdles

  • Medical necessity / prior auth — the procedure must be justified (failed conservative care, imaging, function).
  • Inpatient-Only list & Two-Midnight Rule — TKA came off the IPO list in 2018, THA in 2020. Billing an "uncomplicated" case as inpatient is now a recoupment risk.
  • Site-of-service policies — commercial payers increasingly deny inpatient admission for routine joints, forcing outpatient/ASC.
  • NCCI bundling edits & global periods — coding errors get claims denied or downcoded.
The big 2026 shiftThe mandatory TEAM bundled-payment model began January 1, 2026, putting ~741 hospitals across 188 metro areas at financial risk for the full 30-day episode of lower-extremity joint replacement, spinal fusion, and hip-fracture care. It pulls forward the same incentive Mako already serves — fewer complications, shorter stays — but also squeezes per-case spend, which is a double-edged sword for premium-priced implants and capital.
Nuance to pressure-test

CMS has cited average total joint episode spend roughly in the $16,500–$33,000 range, and a single TKA can carry $11,000–$30,000 in allowed charges depending on site and payer. Question: should the field lead with "Mako helps you win under TEAM" (operational efficiency) or avoid the bundled-payment topic entirely with capital-sensitive administrators? This is a positioning decision, not a fact — flagging for sales leadership.

Geography

United States

The core market and where Mako is most deeply penetrated. Access is shaped less by "is it covered" and more by site-of-service migration (hospital → ASC), bundled payment, and the capital-budget cycle. Distribution is largely direct sales force.

Growth runway

International

Mako has been used in 45+ countries and 1.5M+ procedures globally, but penetration outside the US is lower — a key tailwind. The honest weather report

Scary headwinds & exciting tailwinds

⚠ Headwinds

Bundled payment compression (TEAM)

Mandatory 30-day episode risk from Jan 2026 pressures per-case spend and complicates premium implant/capital justification.

No reimbursement for the robot

Robotic assistance carries no separate payment code — the entire ROI rests on operational savings that skeptical CFOs may discount.

Price competition in robotics

J&J's VELYS has been positioned well below Mako's price; cheaper, image-free systems lower the barrier for switchers.

ASC margin & capital pressure

Outpatient migration shifts purchasing power to cost-sensitive ASCs with tighter capital budgets and less tolerance for $1M robots.

Tariffs & supply costs

Stryker flagged a net tariff impact of roughly $175M for 2025 — a real input-cost headwind on hardware.

Pricing & PROM small-numbers risk

Modestly negative pricing on implants; hospitals report mandatory patient-reported outcomes where low case counts can swing scores.

↗ Tailwinds

Demographics & OA prevalence

An aging population with rising osteoarthritis steadily expands the joint-replacement pool — a durable secular driver.

Razor-and-blade installed base

1.5M+ Mako procedures across 45+ countries; each placed robot pulls through implants and a service annuity for years.

New applications expand the TAM

Mako Spine, Shoulder, hip revision and the Mako RPS open whole new procedure categories on the same platform.

Efficiency story fits value-based care

If Mako genuinely cuts length-of-stay and complications, it becomes the answer to TEAM rather than a victim of it.

Inari + Vascular high-growth entry

Roughly $4.9B bet opens the fast-growing peripheral VTE segment, diversifying away from elective-procedure cyclicality.

International & digital runway

Lower ex-US robot penetration plus the SmartHospital/care.ai recurring-software layer offer growth beyond hardware.

Mako (SmartRobotics) Product

Stryker's robotic-arm-assisted surgery platform. Uses a pre-operative CT to build a 3D plan, then constrains the surgeon's instrument within haptic boundaries during the cut.

Sales angle: the anchor of the orthopaedic franchise — capital sale today, implant & service annuity for years. Bio impact: none directly; it improves the precision of the implant procedure.

Mako 4 / Mako RPS Product

Mako 4 is the fourth-generation single platform spanning hip, knee, partial knee and spine. Mako RPS is a newer system aimed at manual surgeons who don't use the arm-based robot.

Sales angle: RPS widens the funnel to lower-volume and price-sensitive accounts without cannibalizing the premium robot.

Triathlon Product

Stryker's flagship total-knee implant system, built around a single-radius femoral design intended for kinematic stability through the range of motion.

Sales angle: highest-volume implant; the blade to Mako's razor. Bio impact: restores load-bearing joint mechanics, relieving osteoarthritis pain.

Triathlon Gold Product

A 3D-printed femoral component with a titanium-nitride coating for patients with metal sensitivities, available cemented or cementless.

Sales angle: neutralizes the "nickel allergy" objection that historically sent cases to competitors.

Inari Medical Product

Acquired in Feb 2025 (~$4.9B). Maker of FlowTriever and ClotTriever mechanical thrombectomy devices for venous thromboembolism. Now part of the Vascular business.

Sales angle: entry into a fast-growing, less elective-cyclical segment. Bio impact: physically removes blood clots, potentially avoiding thrombolytic bleeding risk.

ProCuity Product

Stryker's connected/wireless hospital bed line within the Medical business, aimed at fall prevention and caregiver workflow.

Sales angle: safety + labor-efficiency story for hospital operations buyers.

LIFEPAK Product

Defibrillator/monitor line for emergency and acute care, part of the Medical business.

Sales angle: trusted brand in EMS and code-cart settings; recurring consumables/pads.

Vocera Product

Clinical communication and workflow platform (wearable badges, software) acquired by Stryker, now part of the digital/Medical layer.

Sales angle: recurring software revenue and a hook into hospital IT.

SmartHospital Platform Product

Digital-health platform launched March 2026, integrating ambient AI room monitoring (from care.ai) with Stryker's communication and workflow tools.

Sales angle: shifts mix toward recurring software; differentiates on operations, not just devices.

mild (Vertos) Product

Minimally invasive lumbar decompression — an outpatient interventional procedure for lumbar spinal stenosis, gained via the Vertos Medical acquisition.

Sales angle: outpatient/ASC-friendly interventional option. Bio impact: removes tissue compressing the spinal canal to relieve nerve symptoms.

Neptune Product

Surgical waste-fluid management system within Instruments.

Sales angle: disposables annuity plus OR safety/efficiency.

Endoscopy / 1788 Product

Minimally invasive visualization business; the 1788 platform provides 4K and fluorescence imaging for the OR.

Sales angle: capital + recurring scopes/consumables; integrates with ICG fluorescence.

Q Guidance Product

Stryker's surgical guidance/navigation system, integrated with Mako 4 and used in spine procedures.

Sales angle: precision and planning value-add layered on capital.

AccuStop Product

The haptic technology in Mako Spine that holds the robotic arm within a pre-planned trajectory boundary.

Sales angle: tangible "the robot keeps you on plan" demonstration point.

Enabling Technologies Product

Umbrella for navigation, robotics and related capital reclassified into Other Orthopaedics in the 2024 reorganization.

Sales angle: the "platform" layer that differentiates the implant sale.

Mako Shoulder Product

Robotic-assisted shoulder arthroplasty application, including reverse procedures, in limited market release.

Sales angle: new procedure category on an installed robot — incremental pull-through.

Capital equipment / ASP Sales / cost

"Capital" is the large one-time purchase (the robot itself); ASP is its average selling price — for ortho robots roughly $0.5M to over $1M in the US.

Sales angle: the capital sale opens the account; the recurring implants/service are where the margin compounds.

Pull-through (razor-and-blade) Sales / cost

The model where a placed capital device drives recurring sales of the implants, disposables and service that go with it.

Sales angle: justifies discounting the robot to lock in years of higher-margin recurring revenue.

TAM (Total Addressable Market) Sales / cost

The total revenue opportunity if a product captured 100% of its relevant market.

Sales angle: used to size new applications (e.g., Mako Spine expands TAM beyond hip/knee).

Revision Clinical / bio

A second surgery to replace or repair a previously implanted joint that has failed, worn, or loosened. Mako now offers a first-to-market robotic hip-revision capability.

Sales angle: revisions are complex and higher-value; robotic guidance is a differentiator. Bio impact: restores function after implant failure.

510(k) Regulatory / payer

An FDA premarket notification pathway showing a device is "substantially equivalent" to an already-marketed one. Faster than full PMA approval; results in "clearance," not "approval."

Sales angle: why most device line-extensions reach market quickly without pharma-scale trials.

PMA Regulatory / payer

Premarket Approval — the FDA's most stringent pathway, for higher-risk (Class III) devices, requiring clinical evidence of safety and effectiveness.

Sales angle: a PMA is a higher barrier to entry and can be a competitive moat.

MS-DRG Regulatory / payer

Medicare Severity Diagnosis-Related Group — the fixed inpatient payment a hospital receives for a given case type (e.g., DRG 469/470 for major joint replacement). The implant is paid out of this fixed amount.

Sales angle: explains why a pricier implant directly squeezes hospital margin — there's no extra reimbursement for it.

APC Regulatory / payer

Ambulatory Payment Classification — the outpatient/facility payment system that pays for procedures done in hospital outpatient departments and, via related rates, ASCs.

Sales angle: the payment context as joints migrate out of the inpatient setting.

Inpatient-Only (IPO) list Regulatory / payer

CMS list of procedures Medicare will only pay for as inpatient. Total knee (27447) was removed in 2018 and total hip (27130) in 2020, enabling outpatient/ASC joint replacement.

Sales angle: the regulatory trigger behind the entire site-of-service migration to ASCs.

Two-Midnight Rule Regulatory / payer

Medicare guidance that inpatient admission is appropriate only when care is expected to span two midnights; otherwise the case should be outpatient.

Sales angle: billing uncomplicated joints as inpatient is now a recoupment risk — pushes cases outpatient.

TEAM Regulatory / payer

Transforming Episode Accountability Model — CMS's mandatory bundled-payment program that began Jan 1, 2026, holding selected hospitals at financial risk for the full 30-day episode (incl. joint replacement, spinal fusion, hip-fracture).

Sales angle: double-edged — rewards the efficiency Mako claims to deliver, but squeezes per-case spend on premium implants/capital.

PROM Regulatory / payer

Patient-Reported Outcome Measure — standardized patient-reported scores increasingly required for joint-replacement quality reporting and tied to reimbursement.

Sales angle: tools that demonstrably improve outcomes help hospitals hit reporting benchmarks.

NCCI edits Regulatory / payer

National Correct Coding Initiative — CMS rules defining which procedure codes can't be billed together; updated quarterly.

Sales angle: a coding/denial reality that affects whether the facility actually gets paid cleanly.

ASC Regulatory / payer

Ambulatory Surgery Center — an outpatient facility, increasingly where primary joint replacements are performed.

Sales angle: the fastest-growing channel; demands smaller footprint, lower cost, fast turnover.

Osteoarthritis (OA) Clinical / bio

Degenerative joint disease in which cartilage breaks down, causing pain and stiffness — the primary indication driving hip/knee replacement.

Bio impact: the underlying disease the implant treats. Sales angle: aging demographics steadily expand this pool.

Haptic Product

Force/tactile feedback. Mako uses haptic boundaries to physically resist moving the instrument outside the planned region.

Sales angle: the most tangible demo of "the robot keeps the surgeon on plan."

CT-based planning Clinical / bio

Mako requires a pre-op CT scan to build a patient-specific 3D model for surgical planning (vs. some image-free competitor systems).

Sales angle: precision selling point — but competitors counter that image-free avoids extra scan cost/radiation.

Osseointegration Clinical / bio

The biological process by which bone grows into a porous-coated implant surface, fixing it without cement (cementless fixation).

Bio impact: long-term implant stability. Sales angle: cementless options can speed OR time and suit younger, active patients.

Femoral component Clinical / bio

The implant portion that caps the end of the femur in a knee (or articulates as the head in a hip).

Sales angle: material/coating choices (e.g., Triathlon Gold) address patient-specific objections.

Cobalt-chromium (CoCr) Clinical / bio

A hard, wear-resistant alloy used for bearing surfaces like the femoral component.

Bio impact: durability; but metal sensitivity in some patients drives demand for alternatives like Triathlon Gold.

Titanium-nitride (TiN) coating Clinical / bio

A hard ceramic-like coating (the gold-colored surface on Triathlon Gold) that reduces metal-ion exposure for sensitive patients.

Sales angle: directly answers the nickel-allergy objection.

UHMWPE Clinical / bio

Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene — the plastic bearing surface between metal components in a joint implant.

Bio impact: wear debris from PE is a historic cause of implant loosening; material advances reduce it.

Highly-crosslinked PE (XLPE) Clinical / bio

A processed polyethylene engineered for lower wear, extending implant longevity.

Sales angle: longevity story, relevant under bundled payment's revision-cost exposure.

Ceramic bearing Clinical / bio

A very hard, smooth bearing material (often used for hip heads) prized for low wear.

Sales angle: premium option for younger/active hip patients.

Mechanical thrombectomy Clinical / bio

Physically removing a blood clot with a catheter/device (aspiration or extraction), as Inari's FlowTriever/ClotTriever do.

Bio impact: restores blood flow; can avoid the bleeding risk of clot-dissolving drugs. Sales angle: core to the Vascular growth thesis.

VTE Clinical / bio

Venous thromboembolism — the umbrella term for deep vein thrombosis (DVT) and pulmonary embolism (PE).

Sales angle: large, under-treated market entered via Inari.

DVT Clinical / bio

Deep vein thrombosis — a clot, typically in a leg vein, that can break loose and travel to the lungs.

Bio impact: untreated, can cause life-threatening PE.

PE Clinical / bio

Pulmonary embolism — a clot lodged in the lung's arteries; can be rapidly fatal.

Bio impact: high-acuity; rapid clot removal can be life-saving.

Thrombolytic / lysis Clinical / bio

Clot-dissolving drugs (e.g., tPA) or catheter-directed lysis — the pharmacologic alternative to mechanical clot removal.

Sales angle: mechanical thrombectomy is positioned to avoid the bleeding risk these carry.

Acute ischemic stroke Clinical / bio

Stroke caused by a clot blocking brain blood flow — addressed by neurovascular/neuro-interventional devices.

Bio impact: time-critical; faster reperfusion preserves brain tissue.

Lumbar spinal stenosis Clinical / bio

Narrowing of the spinal canal in the lower back that compresses nerves, causing pain/weakness — the target of the mild procedure.

Bio impact: decompression relieves nerve symptoms.

CMF Clinical / bio

Craniomaxillofacial — implants/fixation for skull and facial reconstruction, within Neuro Cranial and Trauma & Extremities.

Sales angle: specialized, defensible niche.

Surgical navigation Product

Computer-assisted tracking that shows instrument position relative to anatomy in real time — distinct from robotics but often paired.

Sales angle: precision add-on; lower-capital entry than a full robot.

ICG fluorescence Clinical / bio

Indocyanine-green fluorescence imaging used with endoscopy systems to visualize blood flow/perfusion intraoperatively.

Sales angle: differentiates the 1788 visualization platform.
